Product and quotient of fractions

SA: "I don't know half of you half as well as I should like...

En Català!

...and I like less than half of you half as well as you deserve.” 

Probably, with the second part of this sentence, Mr. Baggins (Bolsón) was referring to the part of my family that, when I ask them how much cake they want, they say: "Half for me!" And then, just as I start cutting it, "No! Half of the half! Or even less! A third!" And so on, infinitely.

Luckily, I managed to escape—until my uncle caught me to ask whether we needed more wine. "If each bottle is 700ml, that's seven-tenths of a liter, and we have four bottles, that makes twenty-eight tenths, but each glass only holds two-fifths... How many glasses can we still serve? You know we still have to account for the whole Ibizan side of the family..."

As Jean-Paul Sartre said, hell is other people. But that must have been because he didn’t know about fractions!

So, what would you say it means to calculate half, or a third, or even three-quarters of something? And if that "something" is already a fraction, what should we do?

Multiply in lines, divide in crosses

If you think what multiplying and dividing means, we can conjure up a rule:       

MULTIPLY⇨  multiply in LINE,
DIVIDE ⇨ multiply in CROSS. Check it! We do both operations (even the division) with a multiplication! We just need to rearrange numbers!

\[  \frac a b \cdot \frac c d = \frac{a \cdot c}{b \cdot d}  \quad \quad \quad \quad  \frac a b : \frac c d = \frac{a \cdot d}{b \cdot c} \]

Dividing is multiplying by the inverse

The inverse of a fraction, which we can denote like a power (to the  -1), is the same fraction inversing numerator and denominator.

\[ \left( \frac a b \right)^{-1} \]

Therefore, dividing by a fraction is the same as multiplying by its inverse.

\[    \frac a b : \frac c d = \frac a b \cdot   \left(\frac c d \right)^{-1} = \frac a b \cdot \frac d c = \frac{a \cdot d}{b \cdot c} \]
