Sorting fractions

SA: Where is it going? Ordering cans and also fractions.

En Català!

At the party, we were given several ingredients to make a special recipe. The ingredients are: salt, flour, sugar, baking soda, wheat starch, yeast, ingredient X (with lots of love), and other things.

In the kitchen space, we have a series of containers of different sizes to measure the ingredients, and we want to use the smallest one for the substance with the least volume. The problem is that, since we have adapted the recipes, all the ingredient quantities are presented as fractions. This means that, for example, we might need 1/15 of a liter of salt, 3/4 of a liter of flour, 4/5 of sugar, 2/15 of baking soda, 1/8 of wheat starch, 2/15 of yeast... The other quantities are also all in fractions.

What do we have? Some comparisons seem simple. We have the same amount of baking soda as yeast \( \left( \frac 1 {15} \right) \). Also, there is more of these two than of salt \( \left( \frac 2 {15} \right) \). We can even compare salt and wheat starch \( \left( \frac 1 {8} \right) \), where it makes sense that, because fewer portions were made, each portion of wheat starch should be bigger. But what about sugar and flour? Or flour and yeast?

What will we place in the smallest container? And the next one? And all the others?


How to know if one fraction is greater than another?

When we have a graphical representation of the fractions, we can decide at a glance (by seeing which has more quantity or which is further to the right on the number line). But it might be that we don’t have this representation.

It would also be easy to compare them if we knew the decimal expression of each fraction. But we usually don’t know it, and we’d have to do the divisions with decimals.

However, by using the properties of fractions, we can save some work.
We will do it like this:

  • Fractions with the same denominator: the order is the same as the numerators.
  • Fractions with the same numerator: they are ordered in reverse order of the denominators.
              (*) Think about why 14\frac{1}{4}41​ is greater than 13\frac{1}{3}31​: If we divide (for example) 24€ among 3 children, each one will get more money than if we divide it among 4!
  • General case: reduce to a common denominator (LCM of the denominators)
               After doing this, all the fractions will have the same denominator, and we are in the first case. Now we compare the numerators.
