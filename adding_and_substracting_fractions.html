Adding and substracting fractions

SA: "This one stays, that one goes, this one's mine, and down it goes!"

En Català!

During the party, my cousin's youngest son, Xavi, started feeling unwell. At first, he had a lot of energy, then he felt stuffed, and finally, he became sleepier than ever. A while later, after talking it over, we found out what had happened: he had eaten too much cake and consumed too much sugar.

My uncle only ate half of his slice and it seems that Xavi asked him for the other portion. From my aunt, who had only eaten a third of her slice, he took the rest. And my sister said she had cut two portions into five parts for herself, her husband, and their three children, but two pieces were left over, which Xavi also ate. However, she told us that from those last pieces, she gave a third to her brother.

How much cake might Xavi have eaten?

How can we add or substract fractions?

Adding or substracting fractions is really easy whenever are divided in the same pieces (same denominator), because they're all of the same kind. Example: If we have 8 fourths plus 3 fourths, the result is just 11 fourths. In here, fourths is kind of a last name, nothing else, and it does not change. 

\[ \frac 8 4 + \frac 3 4 = \frac {8+3}{4} = \frac {11}{ 4}  \]

Therefore, when we have to add or substract fractions, first we need them to have all the same denominator. To do so, we'll do the same as when we ordered fractions, reduction to common denominator. Later, we'll add or substract the numerators, as before.

At last, the summary of this algorythm is this:

1. If all fractions have the same denominator, move to point 3.
2. If they have different denominators, do reduction to common denominator:
   a. Calculate mcm (lcm) of all denominators.
   b. Calculate equivalent fractions with the same denominator.
3. Add/Substract numerators & copy denominators.
4. Simplify.

Are you doing it right?

Be careful—it's very common to make the mistake of adding numerators with numerators and denominators with denominators. This operation is completely wrong.

  • Remember that you can only add or subtract fractions with the same denominator. After that, you can operate with the numerators.
  • As always, you should simplify the result at the end.
