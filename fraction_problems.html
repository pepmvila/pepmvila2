Fraction problems

Problems and more problems

En Català!

By luck or misfortune, mathematics is not often found in its natural state in the real world. Usually, it appears somewhat hidden. Almost wholly, we can see it as the operations we must perform to reach a series of conclusions. But, if we manage to read between the lines, rationally, we will be able to see what we are looking for, whatever that may be.  FRACTIONS. In such a subtle way that we didn't even realize it. We're probably not aware of it, but if we pay attention, we might find it. AND MORE FRACTIONS. It can become very complicated, but I need to say that I believe in you. Here's hoping. TONS OF FRACTIONS.

Below you'll find a recollection of interactive problems to solve, using all knowledge acquired during this entire unit.

You'll add points as you solve them. Be careful because original data is random, it does not repeat.

The first set of activities focus each on a certain part of the unit. However, the last one has to do with all sections at the same time.

You'll practice the following:

  • Which part is it? Fraction concept..
  • Calculate the total knowing a part of it. Reflect on the concept of fractions and calculate the fraction of a number..
  • Adding, substracting and comparing, plus applications of all of these. 
  • Product and division.
  • Be careful! Each kind of problem (situation) does not correspond to one specific kind of opearation.
  • Combined: Test if you've really understood how to solve the different situations. Each exercise will propose a different problem, in which you need to use all prior knowledge.

Fractions. Parts and Totals

In fraction problems, we often use different words that are interconnected. We can see them in the following example:

Grandma has found a recipe she wants to make, but it's for twelvepeople. Since it's too much (she only wants to make it for nine), she has taken only a fraction of each quantity, three-quarters.
In this case, twelve is the total number of people, which we will call T. From this total, we take only nine, which is the part, referred to as P. As we mentioned, this represents three-quarters, the fraction F that Grandma has made.

There are three formulas (though really, it's just one where elements are moved around):

  • If we want to calculate the fraction (knowing the part & total):

 \[ \boxed {\color{orange} F \color{black} = \frac P  T} \]

  • If we want to calculate the part (knowing the fraction & total): 

 \[ \boxed { \color{blue} P \color{black} = F \ de \  T = F \cdot  T} \]

The phrase reads: Three-quarters of twelve people are nine.

  • If we want to calculate the total (knowing the part & fraction): 

\[ \boxed {\color{ForestGreen} T \color{black} =  P : F} \]

Here it is important to make sure that the fraction and the part refer to the same thing (For example, if I say that three-quarters of the class passed, the part should be the ones who passed, not the ones who failed).
